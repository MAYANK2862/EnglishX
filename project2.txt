1. Executive Summary
Zenith School of AI runs a communication course for learners whose native language is Hindi. Most of
them can read and understand English well, but freeze when they have to speak it — there is simply
no safe, judgement-free place to practise. Group classes give each learner a few minutes of speaking
time at best, and feedback on pronunciation, vocabulary, and grammar is generic rather than
personal.
This project is a voice-first AI English speaking coach. A learner opens the app and has a spoken
conversation with an AI partner — free talk, role-plays (job interview, self-introduction, ordering at a
restaurant), or picture description. After every session the app returns structured feedback on three
dimensions: pronunciation (mispronounced words, pace, clarity), vocabulary (range, repetition,
better word choices), and grammar (errors with corrections and the underlying rule). Performance
rolls up into a defined competency level (Levels 1–6, mapped to CEFR A1–C2) per dimension.
The product has two sides. The learner side is the practice app. The admin side is for Zenith's
course instructors: they invite students by email, organise them into batches, and watch a dashboard
of each student's competency levels, practice frequency, and improvement over time. Technically, the
build follows the mandated stack: two Dockerised microservices (Express.js + FastAPI with
LangGraph), a Next.js frontend, PostgreSQL, custom JWT auth, AWS SES, EC2 deployment with
NGINX and HTTPS, CI/CD, and OpenTelemetry. Section 9 maps every requirement to a concrete
use case.
2. Problem Statement
2.1 Target Users
Learners: Hindi-native students and early professionals (18–30) enrolled in Zenith School of AI's
communication course. They understand English but lack speaking practice and confidence; many
have never received specific feedback on their spoken English.
Admins/Instructors: Zenith's communication coaches who need to onboard batches of students,
ensure they practise regularly, and see objective competency data instead of impressions from
occasional class interactions.
2.2 Pain Points
•
No speaking practice: Learners consume grammar lessons and videos but almost never speak
English aloud — the one skill interviews and workplaces actually test.
•
Fear of judgement: Practising with peers or teachers is intimidating; mistakes feel public. An AI
partner removes the social cost of errors.
•
Generic feedback: "Work on your fluency" is not actionable. Learners need the exact
mispronounced words, the exact grammatical errors, and the corrections.
•
No measure of progress: Without defined levels, learners cannot tell whether a month of effort
moved them anywhere.

3. Product Vision
"A patient English-speaking partner in your pocket — one that never judges, hears every sentence
you say, tells you exactly what to fix, and shows your teacher how far you've come."
The product combines three capabilities: conversation practice (voice-first AI partner with
scenarios), diagnostic feedback (pronunciation, vocabulary, grammar with specific corrections), and
competency tracking (levelled scoring visible to both learner and instructor).

6. Core Features
6.1 Voice Conversation Practice
•
Free talk mode: open conversation with the AI partner on everyday topics; the agent keeps the
conversation going with follow-up questions matched to the learner's level
•
Scenario role-plays: guided situations — self-introduction, HR interview, ordering food, phone
call to customer support, describing your project
•
Picture/prompt description: the learner speaks for 60–90 seconds on a shown image or topic
card
•
Voice-first UX: push-to-talk or continuous mode; the AI replies with voice (TTS) and an
on-screen transcript; the learner can replay their own audio
•
Level-aware partner: the agent adapts its own vocabulary and speaking pace to the learner's
current level, stretching them slightly beyond it

10. System Architecture (High Level)
10.1 Components
•
Frontend (Next.js): learner voice UI (recorder, transcript, TTS playback, reports) and admin
dashboards. Talks only to ms1's public API; audio uploads may go directly to ms2 via the NGINX
route with the same JWT.
•
ms1 — Core API (Express.js): auth with role claims, invites and batches, session records, level
history, dashboard aggregation, SES email dispatch. Owns the Postgres schema.
•
ms2 — Speech & Agent Service (FastAPI + LangGraph): speech-to-text (faster-whisper local
or a cloud STT API), the conversation-partner graph, the three-dimension feedback pipeline,
scoring, and TTS for the AI's voice replies.
•
PostgreSQL: single source of truth; audio stored on S3 or EC2 disk with references in the DB.
•
NGINX: TLS termination, routing, large-body handling for audio.
•
OpenTelemetry → Jaeger (or hosted): traces across the voice loop.
10.2 Key Flows
•
Invite flow: admin (frontend) → ms1 creates tokenised invites → SES emails → student signs
up via link → bound to batch.
•
Practice flow: learner audio → ms2 STT → conversation graph replies (text + TTS) → turns
accumulate → session end triggers the feedback pipeline → scores persist via ms1 →
dashboards update.
•
Digest flow: nightly job in ms1 aggregates batch stats → SES weekly digest to instructors.

11. MVP Scope — 2 Weeks
The MVP must prove the core loop end-to-end on live infrastructure: admin invites a student →
student has a voice conversation → receives three-dimension feedback → levels update →
admin sees it on the dashboard. Cut breadth aggressively, but do not cut the mandatory technical
requirements — deployment, auth with roles, both microservices, LangGraph, SES, and observability
are all in the 2-week scope.
11.1 In Scope
•
Two conversation modes only: free talk + one role-play (self-introduction / HR interview)
•
Invite-based signup (single email invites; CSV bulk can wait) + JWT auth with learner/admin
roles
•
Voice session: record → STT → AI partner reply (text on screen; TTS if time permits) for 5–10
turn conversations
•
Post-session feedback report covering all three dimensions, generated by the LangGraph
pipeline (pronunciation via word-level STT confidence + common Hindi-speaker patterns is
acceptable at MVP)
•
Simple placement conversation and Level 1–6 scoring per dimension with rolling averages
•
Learner dashboard (level cards + session history) and admin dashboard (student list with levels
and last-practised date)
•
Daily practice reminder email via SES
•
Full mandatory infra: monorepo, Docker, CI/CD, EC2 + NGINX + certbot + domain, basic OTel
traces on the voice loop
11.2 Out of Scope (Post-MVP)
CSV bulk invites and multi-batch management. Phoneme-level pronunciation scoring and
record-back drills. Inline (mid-conversation) corrections. Batch-level error analytics and weekly
instructor digests. Streaks and gamification. Additional role-play scenarios and picture description.
Google OAuth. Audio replay library. Fix-list re-practice drills.

12. Learning Outcomes
•
Building a voice pipeline end-to-end: browser audio capture, streaming/uploading,
speech-to-text, and TTS responses
•
Designing multi-node LangGraph pipelines — a stateful conversation agent and a parallel
analysis fan-out — rather than single prompt calls
•
Implementing role-based custom JWT auth and a secure invite-token flow
•
Designing a scoring system: rubrics, rolling averages, and level transitions that feel fair to users
•
Shipping production infrastructure: Docker, NGINX (with large-body audio handling), HTTPS,
domains, CI/CD, SES
•
Using OpenTelemetry to find and fix real latency problems in a user-facing voice loop

15. Open Questions
•
STT choice: self-hosted faster-whisper (free, needs CPU/GPU headroom on EC2) vs a cloud
STT API (accurate word-level confidence, per-minute cost)? Document the trade-off you pick.
•
How accurate can pronunciation feedback be from STT confidence alone, and when do you
need a dedicated pronunciation-assessment model?
•
Should inline corrections interrupt the conversation, or does interrupting damage the confidence
the product exists to build?
•
How should levels map to CEFR honestly without over-claiming ("Level 4 ≈ B2-ish" vs a certified
score)?
•
Latency budget: what is the maximum acceptable delay between the learner finishing a sentence
and the AI's reply — and which stage (upload, STT, LLM, TTS) gets optimised first?
